### For the Community and More…

<details style="margin-bottom: 20px; border: 1px solid #e1e4e8; border-radius: 6px; padding: 10px;">
  <summary style="cursor: pointer; font-weight: bold; font-size: 1.1em;">
    The Human Library, UWaterloo Version
    <small>May 2025, associated with being a Connection Lead</small>
  </summary>
  
  <div style="padding-top: 10px;">

    <p><b>Summary</b></p>
    <ul><li>Initiated and led a Human Library–style event at UWaterloo, pitching the concept, coordinating stakeholders, and designing an inclusive, accessible space for open dialogue and community engagement.</li>
    <li>Gained hands-on experience in event strategy, stakeholder buy-in, marketing, user-centered design, and leading cross-functional teams to execute a high-impact initiative.</li></ul>

    <p><b>The Long Story</b></p>

    <p> In February 2024, I found the inspiration for this project while doomscrolling on Instagram. I came across a reel about an event hosted in Denmark, and its motto to "unjudge someone" deeply resonated with me. In a world where peace feels fragile and people are quick to distrust others—relying ever more on the very stereotypes we have tried so hard to break down over the decades—I found this concept profound and more necessary than ever. I thought, why don't we host our own UWaterloo version? I quickly pitched the idea to my supervisor, and in many ways my mentor and friend, Tara Kleinsteuber. With her support, along with Fantasia Gunn and the rest of the Connection Collective team, I was able to turn this idea into a reality. The goal, taken verbatim from my invitation to our guests, was “to provide a space where open dialogue can challenge assumptions, encourage empathy, and help students connect with perspectives they may not otherwise encounter in their daily lives.” </p>

    <p> We hosted the event in the Black & Gold Room at the SLC on May 22, 2025, inviting members of the community to share their perspectives and engage in the event as "human books." During our two-hour event, pre-registered students and drop-in attendees alike engaged in deep conversations with our guests. When the event came to an end and I had to call for everyone's attention, people felt as if no time had passed at all. Nobody wanted to end their conversations, and many wished the event were longer. As organizers, we cut our closing remarks short after guests voiced a desire to keep talking, and at that moment, I knew the event had achieved its intended purpose. After our final remarks and a brief reflection circle, guests and students shared how the conversations brought out their humanity and empathy—qualities that are not frequently championed in daily life. Follow-up feedback showed that participants left feeling lighter, many gained meaningful connections, and a few even walked away with a new friend! </p>

    <p>I learned many things from building out this idea, including:</p>

    <ul>
    <li> Inclusivity by Design: There are so many details to be mindful of when hosting an event. We prioritized accessibility, flexibility for both short and long conversations, the option to simply listen without pressure to speak, and deep respect for everyone's unique lived experiences. It may challenge your core beliefs, but that is the exact purpose of the event: to challenge those beliefs in a safe environment. </li>
    <li> The Power of Marketing: As with any event, marketing is invaluable for bringing attention to your idea. I learned not to hesitate when asking the right people and groups to help get the word out. </li>
    <li> Localization Matters: Even if an initiative has been successful worldwide, your version will require unique adjustments because every community and set of circumstances is different. </li>
    <li> Atmosphere is Key: I paid a lot of attention to how the room's seating areas were arranged, which is vital for a people-centered event. We prioritized comfortable seating (a non-negotiable), snacks, warm drinks, a light jazz playlist, and ambient natural lighting. We organized tables and chairs to remove the pressure of moving around, giving people the freedom to naturally engage with anyone they wanted to talk to. </li>
    <li> Stripping Away Labels: It was crucial to ensure people treated each other as equals, moving past their daily identities as students, professors, or social workers. </li>
    <li> The Importance of Teamwork: The team you work with to bring an idea to life is invaluable. If your enthusiasm isn't reciprocated, an idea can easily be lost or poorly executed. I am extremely lucky to be surrounded by such an incredible group of people, and I am grateful to Tara for giving me the courage to pursue this. </li>
    </ul>

    <p>If you ever have the opportunity to attend a Human Library, I highly encourage you to take it! Also, if you plan on organizing one and need any help or ideas, feel free to reach out!</p>

<details style="margin-bottom: 20px; border: 1px solid #e1e4e8; border-radius: 6px; padding: 10px;">
  <summary style="cursor: pointer; font-weight: bold; font-size: 1.1em;">
    PCOSight
    <small>Oct 2024, team project</small>
  </summary>

    <b>Summary</b>

    <ul>
    <li> Participated in the Fall 2024 Femtech Innovation Challenge by Velocity, working with biomedical and nanotechnology engineers. </li>
    <li> Built and researched a proposed solution to reduce PCOS diagnosis times in Canada by combining a prototype website with subsidized at-home blood testing and faster specialist matching. </li>
    <li> Led the prototype website and market research; project was presented to physicians and femtech founders and received a special mention from Autocate. </li>
    </ul>

    <p><b>The Longer Version</b></p>

    <b>Background</b>

    <p> I participated in the Femtech Innovation Challenge organized by Velocity in Fall 2024, forming a team with passionate biomedical and nanotechnology engineers. "Femtech" refers to feminine medical technology and its surrounding industry. Prior to this project, I was only vaguely aware of the bias and discrimination against females in medical research and technology. In the beginning, I could only recall the standard example that women are more likely to be fatally injured in car accidents because vehicles are designed and tested for male bodies. However, as I got acquainted with the problem space, I realized that this discrimination isn't only present in the auto industry; it is also glaringly prominent in medical research and healthcare institutions. From prescription medications to clinical research and routine examination devices, women are either left with outdated tools or missing entirely from medical frameworks. </p>

    <p> One major issue is that devices used for women-specific routine examinations are still manufactured using 1930s designs. Furthermore, many other areas of health—such as dementia and PCOS—remain poorly understood or missing from research pipelines because the space is severely underfunded due to taboos, discrimination, and inherent biases that most people ignore. This is exactly one of the systemic issues that Velocity and UWaterloo are trying to bring awareness to. </p>

    <b>Goal</b>

    <p> The goal of our project was to bridge the gap between women and the standard of medical care we rightfully deserve. Our team focused specifically on the flawed diagnosis process for PCOS and other related disorders. The average time it takes to receive a PCOS diagnosis in Canada is 4.3 years. Coupled with the fact that approximately 1 in 10 women face this condition, and that it severely impacts their quality of life—with no proper cure currently available—this became the critical issue my team and I wanted to tackle. </p>

    <b>How it went</b>

    <p>Our project was quite ambitious against the backdrop of the Canadian healthcare system, but we worked hard to navigate the various roadblocks we faced. Our idea was to build a website and home testing kit partially subsidized by OHIP. The home kit would be mailed to the nearest community lab, and the platform would quickly match patients with specialist doctors for a consultation once blood test results came in. I was responsible for building the prototype website and conducting market research to understand existing solutions and position our product effectively. One major challenge we encountered was pricing. We wanted it to be as cost-effective as possible for consumers, especially since our primary target market falls between 18 and 24 years old (the average age of diagnosis in Canada). Ultimately, we presented our project to a panel of judges, including doctors and founders in the femtech space, and even earned a special mention from Autocate!</p>

<details style="margin-bottom: 20px; border: 1px solid #e1e4e8; border-radius: 6px; padding: 10px;">
  <summary style="cursor: pointer; font-weight: bold; font-size: 1.1em;">
    Designing My Own Curriculum for English Tutoring
  </summary>
  
  <div style="padding-top: 10px;">
  <p><b>Background</b></p>

  <p> I tutored english to 3 middle-school-aged students, during which, the primary concerns I encountered were 
  poor reading comprehension, weak analysis skills and an overall low interest in the subject. Upon further digging, I 
  realised that the school curriculum relied on repetitive worksheets and overly generalised learning plans to satisfy broad policy requirements, and it left students feeling disengaged and struggling to develop analytical skills. This was certainly not helped by the general sentiment of STEM subjects holding higher importance. </p>

  <p> I wanted to try something- and these students, who were exhausted with boring english curriculum, were willing to try something new as well. So I decided to implement what I learned from the book, *Visual Intelligence* by Amy E. Herman. </p>

  <p><b>My Approach</b></p>

  <p> Each lesson, we would pick a piece of art, and spend some time to independently think about what the details we noticed. We started with general details like colour, shape, characters, lighting and setting, and slowly moved towards discussing themes and trying to understand what the artist was trying to convey. From here, we started to think about how we could design our writing to explain what we noticed. Paying attention to the nuances of the artwork, we could start to think about how sentence structure, grammar and vocabulary could help make our arguments read well. </p>

  <p><b>The Result! 😁</b></p>
  <p> I adapted lessons dynamically based on student comprehension rather than following a fixed standardized progression. By the end of 6 months, this new curriculum resulted in stronger engagement and faster improvement in reading comprehension and written expression among students! </p>

  <p> This was my first time experiencing joy as a teacher, and I found that I quite liked helping people find their interests in subjects. </p>
